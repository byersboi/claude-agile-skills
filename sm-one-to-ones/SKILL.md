# SM One-to-Ones with Team Members

One-to-ones between a Scrum Master and individual team members are not universally practised — and when they are, they are frequently misunderstood. The SM is not a line manager. They have no appraisal responsibility, no authority over pay or promotion, and no obligation to report individual performance to leadership.

That distinction is the foundation. Without it, 1:1s become either ineffective (the SM doesn't know what they're for) or harmful (the team member doesn't feel safe).

Done well, SM 1:1s are one of the most powerful tools available for understanding what is really happening in a team — the things that never surface in a retrospective, the individual struggles that affect collective performance, the early signals of dysfunction that are easier to address quietly than publicly.

## Why an SM Holds 1:1s

The purpose is not welfare management, not performance oversight, and not information gathering for leadership. The purpose is:

- **Safety and trust**: Building individual relationships so that people feel they have somewhere to bring what they can't say in the room
- **Impediment discovery**: Hearing about blockers, friction, and frustrations that individuals won't raise publicly — often because they feel silly, political, or too personal
- **Individual coaching**: Helping team members think through their work, their relationships with colleagues, or their own growth
- **Team dynamics awareness**: Building a systemic picture of how the team is functioning from multiple perspectives
- **Early warning**: Noticing when someone is struggling, disengaging, or about to leave — before it becomes a crisis

What SM 1:1s are not for:
- Collecting information to report to management
- Managing performance
- Creating dependency on the SM
- Becoming a venting outlet with no action or growth

## Frequency and Format

There is no universal right answer — match the format to the team's context and individual needs.

| Format | When it works |
|---|---|
| Fortnightly, 30 minutes | Stable team, good psychological safety, low turbulence |
| Weekly, 20 minutes | New team, onboarding period, individual going through difficulty |
| Monthly, 45 minutes | Experienced team; individuals with high autonomy |
| Ad hoc only | Mature, high-trust teams where individuals proactively reach out |

**Consistency matters more than frequency**: A fortnightly 1:1 that always happens builds more trust than a weekly one that keeps getting cancelled. Cancelled 1:1s communicate that the individual is low priority.

**In-person vs. remote**: For remote teams, keep the camera on. For co-located teams, consider walking 1:1s — movement reduces the formality and makes difficult conversations easier. Avoid doing 1:1s at a desk where the power dynamic (SM behind a screen) can feel evaluative.

## Agenda Ownership

The team member owns the agenda. This is the most important structural principle of an effective 1:1.

An SM-owned agenda ("here's what I want to cover") signals that the conversation is for the SM's benefit, not the individual's. It also removes the psychological safety that makes 1:1s valuable.

**At the start of every session**: "What's on your mind? What would make this time useful for you?"

If the team member says "nothing, really" — this usually means one of three things:
1. They don't yet trust the relationship enough to bring real things
2. They genuinely are fine and a shorter conversation is appropriate
3. They don't understand what 1:1s are for

Build trust with consistency over time. Don't push for depth before it's there.

**The SM's items**: If you have something to raise, ask permission and put it at the end. "Is there space to talk about something I've noticed?" The team member's topics come first.

## What to Talk About

**Common topics that arise naturally**:
- How they're finding the work — engaging, frustrating, unclear?
- Relationships with teammates — friction, collaboration that's working well
- How they feel about the team's current direction or pace
- Things they wish were different but haven't said publicly
- Their own development — skills they want to grow, things they find challenging
- Things that are blocking them individually that haven't made it to the impediment log
- How they're feeling in general — energy, stress, sense of belonging

**Questions that open things up**:
- "What's been the best part of this sprint for you?"
- "What's been draining you?"
- "Is there anything the team keeps not talking about?"
- "What would make your work easier that nobody's done yet?"
- "How are you finding it working with [colleague]?" (use carefully)
- "What's one thing I could do differently that would help you?"
- "If you could change one thing about how we work as a team, what would it be?"
- "How are you doing — not just at work?"

### Question bank by intent

Use these to purposefully steer a 1:1 depending on what the team member needs — not as a script to run through. Pick one or two that fit the moment.

**Building trust**
- "What's something you've been sitting on but haven't found the right moment to raise?"
- "Is there anything about your work situation that you'd want me to understand better?"
- "What's one thing that's been going well that we haven't stopped to acknowledge?"
- "How supported do you feel when things get difficult in the sprint?"
- "Is there anything I've done — or not done — that's made your job harder?"

**Strengthening the relationship**
- "What's one thing I could do differently that would make me more useful to you?"
- "What's motivating you most about the work right now?"
- "What's changed for you in the last few sprints — in how you're experiencing the team or the work?"
- "Is there something you're proud of recently that hasn't really been noticed?"
- "If you could change one thing about how we work together, what would it be?"

**Uncovering challenges**
- "What's taking longer than it should right now, and what's getting in the way?"
- "Are there any risks or concerns you're holding back from raising because you're not sure they'll land?"
- "Where do you feel like you're waiting on someone else — inside or outside the team?"
- "What feels harder than it ought to be at the moment?"
- "Is there any work happening that feels like it probably shouldn't be — things that seem like waste?"

**Advancing the work**
- "What would make the next sprint meaningfully better than this one?"
- "Is there a quick win available right now that nobody's picked up yet?"
- "What does a really successful sprint look like for you personally?"
- "What would you want the team to protect — things that are working and shouldn't be tinkered with?"
- "If you could fix or change one thing before the end of this sprint, what would it be?"

**What to avoid**:
- Task status updates — that's what standups are for
- Leading questions that reveal what answer you're hoping for
- Fixing things immediately — listen first, ask what they want from you
- Taking on problems that belong to the individual or to the team

## Coaching vs. Pastoral Care

This distinction matters and is easy to blur.

**Coaching** (within SM scope): Helping someone think through a work challenge, a team dynamic, a collaboration difficulty, or their own professional growth. The SM asks questions; the individual finds their own answers.

**Pastoral care** (adjacent to SM scope): Emotional support for someone who is struggling personally — bereavement, mental health, relationship difficulties, burnout. The SM can hold space for this, but should not try to fix it or become the primary support mechanism.

**When pastoral care is needed**:
- Listen fully. Don't rush to solutions.
- Acknowledge what they're going through without minimising it.
- Ask what they need: "Is it helpful to talk about this, or would you rather I just know?"
- Know your limits: if someone is in genuine distress, signpost professional support (EAP, HR, GP). You are not a therapist.
- Follow up: check in between sessions. A brief message carries weight.

**The boundary**: You can be warm, human, and genuinely caring without taking on responsibility for someone's wellbeing that exceeds your capability and role. Blurring this boundary harms both you and them.

## Confidentiality

This is the most important and most commonly mishandled aspect of SM 1:1s.

**The default is confidentiality**: What is said in a 1:1 stays with you unless the individual says otherwise or there is an exceptional circumstance.

**When you can and should act on what you hear**:
- The individual gives explicit permission: "You can raise this in the retro / with the team"
- You hear something that creates a safeguarding or legal obligation (rare, but possible — follow your organisation's policy)
- The individual asks you to take action on their behalf

**The grey area — systemic issues**: If three different people tell you in 1:1s that the same thing is causing problems, you have systemic information that needs to surface — but you cannot attribute it. Create space in a retrospective for the theme to emerge. Don't say "several people told me..." — say "I want to create space to talk about..." The team then surfaces it on its own terms.

**Never**: Report individual 1:1 content to management, use 1:1 information in performance conversations, or share what one team member said about another. Any of these will permanently destroy trust.

**Be explicit about confidentiality early**: At the start of the relationship, name it: "What we talk about here stays between us. If I ever hear something I think we need to act on, I'll ask your permission first."

## 1:1s with Different Team Member Types

**The quiet team member**: Rarely speaks in ceremonies; hard to read. 1:1s are especially valuable here. Ask open questions and sit with silence. Don't interpret quietness as either contentment or unhappiness — find out. They may have observations about the team that the loudest voices never articulate.

**The dominant team member**: Speaks for the team in ceremonies; may not realise their impact. 1:1s are a safe place to explore this. "How do you think others experience you in meetings?" Be specific, not vague. Use SBI for any feedback.

**The disengaged team member**: Going through the motions; low energy. Explore what's beneath it — boredom, frustration, external stress, misalignment with the work? A direct question lands better than dancing around it: "I've noticed you seem less energised lately. What's going on?"

**The venter**: Every 1:1 is a download of complaints. Complaints contain information — listen for themes, patterns, and what's underneath the frustration. But also introduce forward momentum: "That sounds really frustrating. What would you want to be different?" If venting becomes the whole relationship, name it: "I notice we often end up here. I want to make sure we're also finding some traction."

**The person who says "I'm fine"**: May genuinely be fine. Or may not yet trust the relationship. Don't push prematurely. Keep showing up consistently. Over time, "I'm fine" becomes something more.

**The team member who wants to talk about leaving**: Listen without panic. Ask what's driving it. Your job is not to retain them at all costs — it's to help them think clearly. Sometimes the right thing is to leave. Sometimes what they need is a change within the team. Either way, a thoughtful conversation is more useful than reassurance.

## 1:1s with Stakeholders Outside the Team

The Scrum Master's 1:1 relationships are not limited to team members. Regular individual conversations with the line manager, the Product Owner, and leads from other teams are essential to the SM's effectiveness — they provide context the team can't give you, and they build the relationships needed to remove systemic impediments.

These conversations have a different character to team member 1:1s. They are more lateral — the SM is not coaching downward; they are building mutual understanding and alignment with people who have their own accountabilities and pressures.

### 1:1s with Your Line Manager

The SM's manager often holds organisational context, strategic direction, and authority that the SM needs to do their job. This relationship needs to be actively maintained.

**What to cover**:
- How the team is performing overall — not individual detail, but team-level signals
- Impediments that require management authority to resolve
- Your own development — coaching you're seeking, skills you're building
- Concerns about team direction or organisational decisions that affect the team
- Alignment on what you're being asked to do vs. what you believe will help the team most

**Useful questions for manager 1:1s**:
- "What are the biggest concerns you have about this team right now?"
- "Is there anything you're hearing about the team from other leaders that I should know about?"
- "What would success look like for this team in the next quarter, from your perspective?"
- "Is there anything I'm doing that's getting in my own way?"
- "What's the one impediment I could remove that would have the biggest impact on the organisation?"
- "How can I better support you in understanding what the team needs?"

**What to protect**: Do not share individual team member information or 1:1 content. Maintain the boundary clearly. If your manager asks for information about specific individuals, explain your role and why that boundary exists. A manager who understands servant leadership will respect it; one who doesn't is an impediment to investigate.

### 1:1s with the Product Owner

The SM's relationship with the PO is one of the most important in the Scrum system. These two roles need to work in close partnership — and regular 1:1 conversations are often where the real alignment happens.

**What to cover**:
- How the PO is finding their relationship with the team
- Backlog health — refinement happening, items getting ready, Product Goal clarity
- Stakeholder pressures the PO is navigating
- Areas where the SM can coach the team to better support the PO
- Any concerns about team dynamics from the PO's perspective

**Useful questions for PO 1:1s**:
- "How confident are you that the team understands what you need from this sprint?"
- "What's the most frustrating thing about working with the team right now?"
- "Is there anything you wish the team understood better about the stakeholder pressures you're under?"
- "How useful are our refinement sessions for you — are you getting what you need from them?"
- "What's one thing I could do to make your job easier?"
- "Where do you feel most uncertain about backlog priorities — what would help you get more clarity?"

**What to watch for**: A PO who describes the team as "not delivering what I asked for" is often describing a communication or refinement gap rather than a performance problem. A PO who says "the team keeps pushing back" may have unrealistic expectations — or may have a team that needs coaching on constructive challenge. Both are SM focus areas.

### 1:1s with Other Team Leads and Dependency Teams

Cross-team dependencies are one of the most common sources of impediments. Regular 1:1s with the leads of teams your team depends on (or teams that depend on you) maintain the relationships that make dependency management possible.

**What to cover**:
- Current and upcoming dependencies — what each team needs from the other
- Shared ceremonies or touchpoints — are they working?
- Any friction in how the teams collaborate
- Forward visibility — what's coming in future sprints that the other team should know about

**Useful questions for cross-team 1:1s**:
- "Are there things our team is doing that make your team's work harder?"
- "What's the earliest you need to know about work coming from us so you can plan for it?"
- "Is there anything in how we're coordinating that you'd change?"
- "What commitments have we made that you're not confident we'll meet?"
- "How would you describe the working relationship between our teams to someone outside?"

**When this is most valuable**: Before a sprint where dependencies are high; when a team relationship has recently been strained; when an impediment has repeatedly involved the same external team.

## Common Failure Modes

**Becoming the team therapist**: The SM absorbs everyone's stress and becomes the emotional container for the whole team. This leads to SM burnout and team dependency. Maintain boundaries. Signpost other resources. Take your own supervision.

**Creating information asymmetry**: You hold significant information about individuals that the team and leadership do not. This is appropriate — but it shapes how you facilitate. Be careful not to unconsciously use 1:1 knowledge in ways that others can't see or account for.

**Playing favourites**: If 1:1s are only with some team members, or if the quality of relationship with some members visibly differs, it undermines team cohesion. Hold 1:1s consistently across the whole team.

**Making it a status report**: "How's the sprint going?" is not a 1:1. If the conversation never goes deeper than task progress, it is wasting everyone's time and building no trust.

**Fixing instead of listening**: The SM hears a problem and immediately moves to solve it. Resist this. Ask: "What would be most useful — to think through options together, or for me to take something away?" Often people want to be heard, not rescued.

**Skipping when the team seems fine**: The time to invest in individual relationships is when things are going well, not only in crisis. 1:1s in good times build the trust that makes difficult conversations possible later.
