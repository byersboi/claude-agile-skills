# Change Management

Organisational change management (OCM) is the discipline of managing the people side of change — ensuring that individuals understand, accept, and are equipped to adopt a new way of working. It is distinct from managing the technical or operational aspects of a change. A new system can be perfectly built and on time; if the people who need to use it are confused, resistant, or unprepared, the change fails.

Research consistently shows that 70% of change initiatives fail to achieve their intended outcomes. The most commonly cited causes are not technical — they are people-related: insufficient sponsorship, poor communication, unaddressed resistance, and a failure to sustain change once initial momentum fades. Change management exists to address those causes deliberately.

---

## What Is Organisational Change Management?

Change management is not about managing change to a sprint backlog or a release scope. In agile, "change" often means welcoming evolving requirements — that is embraced by design. Organisational change management operates at a different level: it addresses the human experience of significant shifts in how people work, what they are expected to do, and who holds authority.

OCM applies when:
- A new operating model, structure, or way of working is being introduced
- Technology is being replaced or significantly transformed
- An agile transformation is underway
- Roles and responsibilities are changing materially
- Cultural change is required (not just process or tooling)

The fundamental premise is that change is made up of two inseparable components: the technical side (the solution itself — the new process, system, or structure) and the people side (helping individuals move from their current state to the desired future state). Most organisations invest heavily in the technical side and underinvest in the people side. Change management redresses that imbalance.

**Why 70% of change initiatives fail:**

- Sponsors are nominal rather than active — they announce the change but do not visibly role-model it
- Communication is treated as a one-time launch event rather than an ongoing effort
- Middle managers feel bypassed or threatened and become passive resistors
- Employees are told what is changing but not why, and the personal impact is never addressed
- Early resistance is left unaddressed and spreads
- Once the project closes, reinforcement stops and people drift back to old behaviours
- The change is done *to* people rather than *with* them

## Key Frameworks

Three frameworks dominate the field. They are complementary rather than competing — the choice depends on the nature of the change and where support is needed most.

### Prosci ADKAR Model

ADKAR is an individual-change model. It describes the five building blocks a person needs to successfully make a change. A person cannot move to the next stage without achieving the current one — this makes ADKAR a diagnostic tool as much as a planning tool.

| Stage | What It Means | What Failure Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Understanding why the change is happening and why now | "I don't understand why we're doing this" / confusion about the purpose |
| **Desire** | Wanting to support and participate in the change | "I understand it but I don't want it" / passive or active resistance |
| **Knowledge** | Knowing how to change — skills, behaviours, processes | "I want to change but I don't know how" / errors and frustration post-go-live |
| **Ability** | Being able to demonstrate the new behaviour in practice | "I know what to do but can't do it yet" / performance dip after training |
| **Reinforcement** | Sustaining the change over time | Relapse to old ways once pressure eases; lack of recognition or accountability |

**Using ADKAR in practice**: Run an ADKAR assessment for each key stakeholder group. Where is the barrier? If a group scores low on Desire, pouring resources into Knowledge training is wasted. Fix the barrier point first.

### Kotter's 8-Step Model

Kotter's model is an organisational-change model — it describes what the organisation (particularly its leadership) must do to lead change successfully. It is sequential and is best suited to large-scale, top-driven transformations.

1. **Create a sense of urgency** — Make the case for why change is necessary now. If people don't feel urgency, they won't move.
2. **Build a guiding coalition** — Assemble a credible, cross-functional group to lead the change. One leader alone is not enough.
3. **Form a strategic vision and initiatives** — Define where you are going and how you will get there. Keep the vision clear and memorable.
4. **Enlist a volunteer army** — Change at scale requires a critical mass of people actively supporting and spreading the change.
5. **Enable action by removing barriers** — Identify and remove structural blockers: outdated processes, unhelpful incentives, unsupportive managers.
6. **Generate short-term wins** — Create visible, credible early successes. This builds momentum and counters the cynics.
7. **Sustain acceleration** — Use early wins to drive further change; do not declare victory too early.
8. **Institute change** — Embed the change in culture, structures, and systems. Articulate how new behaviours led to success.

**When to use Kotter**: Large, organisation-wide transformations; when senior leadership sponsorship is strong; when a sequential, structured approach suits the culture.

### Lewin's Unfreeze–Change–Refreeze

Lewin's model is the simplest and most foundational. It frames change as moving through three phases:

- **Unfreeze**: Destabilise the status quo. Create the conditions for change by surfacing the problems with the current state. Without unfreezing, people feel no reason to change. This phase requires honest communication about why the current state is unsustainable.
- **Change**: The transition period where new behaviours, systems, and processes are introduced. This is the most uncertain and uncomfortable phase — people are no longer in the old world but not yet comfortable in the new one.
- **Refreeze**: Stabilise the new way of working. Embed it through reinforcement, updated policies, new norms, and recognition. Without refreezing, change does not stick.

**When to use Lewin**: Explaining change to leadership or teams as a mental model; designing interventions at each phase; diagnosing why a change stalled (often, refreezing never happened).

### Choosing the Right Framework

| Situation | Best Fit |
|---|---|
| Diagnosing why individuals are struggling with a change | ADKAR |
| Planning and sequencing an organisation-wide transformation | Kotter |
| Explaining the change journey at a conceptual level | Lewin |
| Running coaching conversations with individuals going through change | ADKAR |
| Designing a change communication and engagement plan | All three inform it |

## Change Curve and Resistance

### The Kubler-Ross Change Curve

Originally developed to describe grief, the Kubler-Ross curve maps closely onto how people experience unwanted organisational change. People do not adopt change instantly — they move through predictable emotional stages, and the sequence matters.

**Stage 1 — Shock/Denial**: "This can't be happening" / "It won't really change." People minimise the change or hope it will go away. Productivity may initially be unaffected because people are not yet engaging with the reality.

**Stage 2 — Anger/Frustration**: Reality sets in. People feel the change is being done to them. Resistance becomes visible — complaints, pushback, vocal opposition.

**Stage 3 — Depression/Low Morale**: The lowest point of the curve. People have accepted the change is real but cannot yet see a positive future. Productivity dips. Disengagement risk is highest here.

**Stage 4 — Acceptance**: People begin to accept the new reality. Not yet enthusiastic, but no longer actively resisting. They start experimenting with new ways of working.

**Stage 5 — Experimentation/Integration**: People explore and adapt. They find what works for them in the new world and begin to build competence.

**Stage 6 — Commitment**: The new way of working becomes normal. People may even advocate for it.

**Using the curve in practice**:
- Not everyone moves through the stages at the same pace; different stakeholder groups will be at different points simultaneously
- Communication and engagement strategies should match the stage, not assume everyone is at Acceptance
- The goal is not to rush people past their feelings but to support them through each stage

### Stages of Resistance

Resistance is not a single thing — it exists on a spectrum and presents differently depending on its root cause.

| Resistance Type | What It Looks Like | Root Cause | Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intellectual** | "This won't work because..." / challenging the logic | Lack of information or genuine disagreement with the approach | Provide data; invite into problem-solving; address specific concerns |
| **Emotional** | Anxiety, worry, visible upset | Fear of loss (status, certainty, relationships, competence) | Acknowledge feelings; provide psychological safety; connect to personal benefits |
| **Political** | Behind-the-scenes manoeuvring; silent in meetings, active blockers outside | Perceived threat to power or influence | Engage directly; involve in design; create a meaningful role in the change |
| **Values-based** | "This contradicts who we are" | Deep cultural or ethical conflict with the change | Explore the underlying values; sometimes the resistance is legitimate feedback |

**Resistance is information.** Before responding to resistance, get curious about its source. Resistance that is dismissed or steamrolled goes underground — it does not disappear.

### Moving People Through the Curve

- **At Shock/Denial**: Communicate clearly and factually. Do not oversell positivity — it breaks trust. Answer "what does this mean for me?" directly.
- **At Anger/Frustration**: Create safe spaces for people to voice concerns. Do not argue or defend. Listen. Use this feedback to improve the change approach.
- **At Depression/Low Morale**: Increase support; make leaders visible and accessible; highlight early wins and small progress; check in individually with team members.
- **At Acceptance and beyond**: Provide training, coaching, and practice opportunities. Recognise and celebrate early adopters. Build on momentum.

## Stakeholder Engagement in Change

### Change Impact Assessment

Before engaging stakeholders, understand what is actually changing for them. A change impact assessment maps the change against the people it affects.

For each affected group, assess:
- What is changing (process, tools, structure, roles, behaviours)?
- How significant is the change? (Scale: Minor / Moderate / Major)
- How ready is the group for this change?
- What support do they need and when?
- Who influences this group's attitude toward the change?

The output is a heat map of impact — showing which groups face the greatest disruption and therefore need the most intensive support.

### Stakeholder Segmentation for Change

Standard stakeholder mapping (Influence vs. Interest) applies here, but for change management it is worth adding a third dimension: **current position on the change**.

| Position | Who They Are | Engagement Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Active Sponsors** | Believe in the change; willing to visibly lead it | Equip, coach, and deploy — use them as visible role models |
| **Passive Supporters** | Positive toward the change but not actively involved | Activate — give them a visible role; help them influence peers |
| **Fence-sitters** | Waiting to see how it lands before committing | Reduce uncertainty; connect them to early adopters; address specific concerns |
| **Quiet Resistors** | Privately opposed but not vocal | Surface concerns in safe settings; do not ignore; they can become blockers |
| **Active Resistors** | Openly opposed; may actively undermine | Engage directly; understand root cause; involve in solution design if possible; escalate if blocking progress |

**Investing disproportionately in active resistors is a common mistake.** Prioritise activating supporters and moving fence-sitters — these efforts generate the most momentum.

### Managing Resistant Stakeholders

The worst approach to resistance is avoidance. Resistant stakeholders who are ignored become resistors who organise.

Steps for engaging a resistant stakeholder:
1. **Seek to understand first**: Meet one-to-one. Ask open questions about their concerns. Listen without defending.
2. **Acknowledge what is legitimate**: Resistance often contains valid feedback. Separating the person's feelings from the content of their concern makes the conversation more productive.
3. **Address the root cause**: Is this about loss of status? Workload? Distrust of leadership? Address the actual source, not the surface objection.
4. **Involve them**: People support what they help create. Give resistant stakeholders a meaningful role in shaping how the change is implemented.
5. **Set clear expectations**: There is a point at which a resistant manager's behaviour becomes a performance issue. That conversation should involve HR and line management, not the change practitioner alone.

### Coalition Building

Kotter's "volunteer army" is built deliberately. A change coalition is a network of people across the organisation who believe in the change and actively help spread it peer-to-peer. Senior leadership can mandate change; peers and local leaders embed it.

Coalition characteristics:
- Represent a cross-section of levels, functions, and geographies
- Are trusted and credible in their own areas — not necessarily the loudest voices
- Are willing to give honest feedback to the change team, not just advocate
- Have been equipped with the knowledge and language to answer colleagues' questions

## Communication in Change

Communication is consistently identified as the most important enabler of successful change — and the one most consistently done badly.

### Change Communication Planning

A change communication plan is not a list of emails. It is a structured approach to ensuring the right messages reach the right people through the right channels at the right time.

Plan components:
- **Audiences**: Who needs to receive this communication? What do they already know and feel?
- **Key messages**: What must each audience know, believe, and do as a result of this communication?
- **Timing**: When does each message need to land? What are the critical moments in the change journey?
- **Channel**: How will this message be delivered? (See below)
- **Sender**: Who is the most credible person to send this message to this audience?
- **Feedback loop**: How will you know if the message was received and understood?

### The Repetition Principle

People do not absorb change communication on first contact. Research suggests that a message needs to be received seven or more times, across different channels, before it is internalised. This is not about repetition for its own sake — it is about reinforcing the same core messages through multiple touchpoints over time.

This is why a single all-hands announcement is never sufficient. It must be followed by team briefings, manager conversations, FAQs, intranet updates, and ongoing reinforcement as the change progresses.

### Channel Selection

| Channel | Best For | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| **All-hands / town hall** | Launching the change; creating urgency; high-visibility moments | One-way; low retention; does not address individual concerns |
| **Team briefings by line manager** | Translating the message to local context; two-way dialogue | Dependent on manager engagement and capability |
| **Intranet / written updates** | Reference information; FAQs; updates over time | Not a substitute for dialogue; low engagement if overused |
| **One-to-one conversations** | Addressing individual resistance; coaching through the curve | Time-intensive; not scalable without a change agent network |
| **Video messages from leaders** | Conveying personal commitment; reaching distributed teams | Can feel staged; works best when authentic and unscripted |
| **Peer-to-peer conversations** | Building trust and normalising the change | Requires a functioning change agent network; hard to script |
| **Working sessions / co-design** | Involving people in shaping the change | Requires genuine intent to act on input |

### Messaging for Different Audiences

The same change lands differently depending on the audience. Tailor messages to what each group cares about:

- **Senior leaders**: Strategic rationale; risk of not changing; their role in sponsoring
- **Middle managers**: What this means for their team; how to support their people; what they need to do differently
- **Front-line employees**: What is changing for me specifically; what support will I get; what happens if I struggle
- **Customers / external partners** (where relevant): What changes for them; continuity of service; who to contact

### Leaders as Communication Channels

The most impactful change communication does not come from the communications team. It comes from respected leaders in the organisation, delivered through genuine conversation rather than scripted cascade. A manager who says "I believe in this change and here is why" carries more weight than a corporate email from the CEO.

Equip leaders with:
- Core messages and talking points (but not a script to read verbatim)
- Time and permission to have conversations, not just broadcast
- Anticipated questions and suggested responses
- Coaching on how to handle resistance in their teams

## Agile Transformation Change Management

Introducing agile is itself a significant organisational change — one with some characteristics that make it particularly challenging to manage.

### Why Agile Transformation Is Uniquely Difficult

- Agile asks people to change not just what they do but how they think — it challenges mental models around control, planning, and predictability
- The change is often abstract until it is experienced; it is hard to communicate what "inspect and adapt" means to someone who has never worked in an agile environment
- Agile distributes authority — which creates explicit winners and losers in terms of organisational power
- The change never "finishes" — there is no cutover date after which agile is done, which makes it hard to celebrate completion

### Managing Loss of Control for Managers

Middle managers in traditional organisations often feel the most threatened by agile transformation. Their value has historically been in planning, controlling, and reporting — all of which change significantly in an agile environment.

Their concerns are legitimate and must be taken seriously:
- "If teams are self-organising, what is my role?"
- "If there are no Gantt charts, how do I give my boss a status update?"
- "What happens to my career path if the hierarchy flattens?"

**Effective responses**:
- Articulate clearly what the new management role looks like — servant leadership, enabling constraints, people development, strategy setting
- Provide genuine development support: coaching, new frameworks, peer learning
- Involve managers in designing how agile will work in their area — do not impose a one-size-fits-all model
- Address career path concerns directly; do not dismiss them

### Helping Teams Embrace New Ways of Working

Teams being asked to work in an agile way face their own change journey. Common barriers:

- **Fear of visibility**: Daily standups and sprint reviews make individual performance visible in ways that feel threatening
- **Uncertainty about role**: Developers, testers, and analysts may feel their professional identity is threatened by cross-functional expectations
- **Scepticism**: "We've seen initiatives like this before; they don't last"
- **Skills gaps**: Agile working requires new skills (facilitation, estimation, product thinking) that take time to develop

**Effective responses**:
- Invest in real training and coaching, not a one-day workshop
- Celebrate early experiments and small wins, even when imperfect
- Create psychological safety for teams to raise concerns — the retrospective is a natural vehicle for this
- Pair teams with experienced agile practitioners during the transition
- Be honest that the transition will involve a performance dip before it improves

### The Role of the Scrum Master in Agile Transformation

In an agile transformation context, the Scrum Master carries an additional change management responsibility: they are on the front line of the people-side of the transformation. They see resistance, confusion, and struggle at the team level daily. They must be equipped to:
- Recognise and name the change curve stages they are observing in their team
- Escalate systemic blockers to the change programme rather than absorbing them
- Work in partnership with organisational change managers, not in isolation

## Sustaining Change

The most common failure mode in change management is not the initial launch — it is the failure to sustain. Once the project is closed and attention moves on, people drift back to what they know.

### Embedding Change in Culture

Culture change is the final frontier of any significant change initiative. Culture is "how we do things around here" — it is embedded in stories, symbols, rituals, and what is rewarded or tolerated. To embed change in culture:

- **Update the stories**: Actively amplify stories of people who are succeeding with the new way of working. Stories are more powerful than policy updates.
- **Align incentives**: If you ask people to collaborate but reward individual performance, the incentive wins. Review performance frameworks, appraisal criteria, and recognition programmes for misalignment.
- **Adjust the symbols**: Physical space, meeting structures, language, and tools all send signals about "what we value here." Change them deliberately to reinforce the new way.
- **Hold people accountable**: If reverting to old behaviours has no consequence, reversion is rational. Leaders who ignore the change must be addressed — quietly if possible, firmly if necessary.

### Reinforcement Mechanisms

Reinforcement is the 'R' in ADKAR and the most neglected. Plan for it explicitly:

- **Celebrating early adopters**: Publicly recognise and celebrate individuals and teams who demonstrate the new behaviours — this signals what is valued and motivates others
- **Sharing progress data**: Make adoption metrics visible. People respond to evidence that the change is working.
- **Leader reinforcement**: Leaders should regularly and visibly reference the change in their communications and decisions. "We made this decision using the new approach and here is what happened" is reinforcement.
- **Peer communities**: Create forums where people doing the new way of working can share experiences, solve problems together, and support each other
- **Coaching support**: Provide ongoing access to coaching during the embedding phase — not just at launch

### Preventing Reversion

Warning signs that reversion is occurring:
- Teams quietly reverting to old tools or processes
- Language shifting back to pre-change terminology
- Low attendance or engagement in new ceremonies or forums
- Performance metrics returning to old patterns
- Leaders stop referencing the change in their communications

When reversion is detected, diagnose before responding. Is this a Knowledge gap (people do not know how to do the new thing)? An Ability gap (they know but cannot perform it yet)? Or a Desire gap (they have chosen not to)? Each requires a different response.

## Measuring Change Adoption

You cannot manage what you do not measure. Change adoption is measurable — it requires intentional design of metrics alongside the change itself.

### Adoption Metrics

| Metric Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Behavioural** | % of teams running retrospectives; % of decisions made using new process; tool login rates |
| **Performance** | Cycle time before and after; error rates; customer satisfaction scores |
| **Attitudinal** | Survey scores on confidence, clarity, and willingness to change |
| **Leading indicators** | Training completion rates; attendance at change events; volume of questions to helpdesk |
| **Lagging indicators** | Business outcomes: cost, quality, speed, customer satisfaction |

Design a mix of leading and lagging indicators. Leading indicators tell you the change is being adopted now. Lagging indicators tell you whether it worked — but too late to course-correct.

### Readiness Assessments

Run readiness assessments before the change launches, not as a post-hoc evaluation. A readiness assessment asks:

- Do stakeholders understand why this change is happening?
- Do they want to support it?
- Do they have the knowledge and skills they need?
- Are the supporting structures (tools, processes, policies) in place?
- Is leadership visibly sponsoring the change?

Score each dimension. Weak readiness in any area is a risk that should be mitigated before the change goes live — not managed reactively after.

### Post-Change Surveys

Run pulse surveys at 30, 60, and 90 days after a significant change lands. Keep them short (5–8 questions). Ask:
- How confident are you in using the new [process/tool/approach]?
- How well do you understand why this change was made?
- What support do you still need?
- On a scale of 1–10, how well is the new way of working in your team?

Use the responses to target follow-up support, not as a report card. The purpose is diagnostic, not evaluative.

### Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

| Leading Indicators | Lagging Indicators |
|---|---|
| Training completion rates | Business performance improvement |
| Attendance at change events | Customer satisfaction uplift |
| Manager engagement scores | Employee turnover reduction |
| Number of questions raised | Process error rate reduction |
| ADKAR scores per group | OKR achievement |

Leading indicators allow you to intervene early. A team with low training completion three weeks before go-live is a predictable risk — addressable now. A team with poor post-launch performance is the same risk, manifested after the fact.

## Change Agent Network

No central change team can touch every person in a large organisation. A change agent network extends the reach of the change programme through trusted peers embedded across the business.

### What a Change Agent Network Is

A change agent network (also called a change champion network) is a group of individuals — typically volunteers, not always senior — who:
- Believe in the change and are willing to actively support it in their area
- Act as a two-way conduit: carrying messages out from the change team and feeding back real concerns and ground-level insight
- Support their immediate colleagues by answering questions, modelling new behaviours, and providing informal coaching
- Represent the change programme credibly because they are one of their peers, not a "change consultant from head office"

### Roles and Responsibilities

| Role | Accountability |
|---|---|
| **Change Lead / Programme** | Overall change strategy; equipping and supporting change agents; consolidating feedback; reporting to sponsors |
| **Sponsor** | Active, visible endorsement; removing organisational blockers; holding leaders accountable |
| **Change Agent / Champion** | Local communication and engagement; peer support; feedback to change team; modelling new behaviours |
| **Line Manager** | Supporting their team through the transition; communicating consistently; addressing individual concerns |

### Selecting Change Agents

Change agents should be selected on credibility and willingness, not seniority. The most effective change agents are:
- Respected by their peers for their expertise or character
- Open to the change themselves — it is very difficult to champion something you do not believe in
- Good listeners and communicators
- Able to manage the dual role of enthusiast and honest feedback conduit without becoming a pure advocate who loses credibility

Avoid appointing change agents purely because they are available or because management has nominated them for political reasons.

### Supporting Change Agents

A change agent network that is poorly supported becomes a liability rather than an asset. Champions who cannot answer questions, who receive inconsistent information, or who feel unsupported will disengage — and their disengagement is visible.

Support mechanisms:
- Regular briefings before major communications land — agents should hear the news before their colleagues, not at the same time
- A shared channel or community where agents can ask questions and share experiences with each other
- Explicit recognition of their contribution — this role is often additional to their day job
- Training in active listening, handling resistance, and change communication
- An honest conversation about what they can and cannot share — agents should know what is confidential

## Anti-Patterns

These are the most common and damaging mistakes in change management. Recognising them early allows you to course-correct before the damage is done.

**Change done TO people, not WITH them**
The change is designed behind closed doors and announced as a fait accompli. People have no opportunity to shape it. Resistance is treated as irrational. This approach generates exactly the resistance it is trying to avoid. Even when the parameters of the change are fixed, involving people in how it is implemented builds ownership and reduces resistance.

**Big-bang cutover without support**
The old system is switched off on a Friday; the new system goes live on Monday. No ongoing support, no managed transition. People are expected to cope. This is a common and avoidable failure. Phased transitions, parallel running where feasible, and a well-staffed support function around go-live significantly reduce the human cost.

**Communication as a one-time event**
A launch email, a town hall, an all-hands — and then silence. People received the message once and should now be on board. This ignores the repetition principle and the reality that people process change at different speeds. Communication must be sustained throughout the change journey, not front-loaded.

**Skipping the 'why'**
The communication leads with what is changing and how, but never adequately addresses why. People cannot commit to something whose purpose they do not understand. The 'why' must be answered before people will engage with the 'what' and 'how'. It must also be answered at a personal level — "why is this good for me?" — not just at an organisational level.

**Ignoring middle management**
Change programmes engage senior leaders (as sponsors) and front-line employees (as recipients), and forget about the layer in between. Middle managers are the single most important group in determining whether change succeeds at team level. They are the daily translators of the change for their people. If they are not bought in, equipped, and supported, they become the most effective resistance point in the organisation.

**Declaring victory too early**
Early signs of adoption are mistaken for embedding. The project closes; the change team moves on; reinforcement stops. Within months, old behaviours return. Sustaining a change requires deliberate, planned reinforcement for a minimum of six to twelve months after go-live — often longer for significant cultural shifts.

**Treating resistance as a problem to eliminate**
Resistance contains information. A stakeholder who says "this will create more work for my team" may be right. Dismissing resistance as an obstacle to be overcome means missing legitimate concerns that, if addressed, would improve the change outcome. Listen to resistance before responding to it.

**Measuring outputs, not outcomes**
Training was delivered; communications were sent; events were attended. Change is declared a success. But the measure that matters is whether people are actually working differently — and whether that difference is delivering the intended results. Confusing activity with impact is a systemic failure in change reporting.
