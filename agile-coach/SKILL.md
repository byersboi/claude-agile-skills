# Agile Coach

An agile coach helps individuals, teams, and organisations improve their agility — the ability to create and respond to change in order to succeed in an uncertain and turbulent environment. Coaching is distinct from training, mentoring, or consulting: it assumes the coachee has the answers and the coach's job is to help them surface and act on those answers.

## Agile Coaching Competency Framework

Developed by Lyssa Adkins and Michael Spayd (Agile Coaching Institute), this framework defines the domains an agile coach must be competent in:

**Foundation**
- Agile-Lean Practitioner: Deep understanding of agile and lean practices from experience

**Primary Competency Domains**
- **Coaching** — Professional coaching skills (ICF-level)
- **Facilitating** — Creating conditions for groups to work productively
- **Mentoring** — Sharing experience to accelerate learning
- **Teaching** — Imparting knowledge and skills in structured ways
- **Technical Mastery** — Deep software craft knowledge (for technical coaches)
- **Business Mastery** — Understanding of business strategy, finance, markets
- **Transformation Mastery** — Change management, organisational design, leadership

Most agile coaches are strong in 2–3 domains. Know your primary stance and develop the others intentionally.

## The Five Coaching Stances

Skilled coaches shift stances based on what the team needs in the moment:

| Stance | Purpose | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Coach** | Unlock answers the team already has | Team is capable but stuck; exploring options |
| **Facilitator** | Create structured space for group work | Group needs to make decisions; tensions present |
| **Mentor** | Share experience to accelerate learning | Team lacks specific knowledge; explicit request |
| **Teacher** | Transfer knowledge systematically | Team needs new concepts or frameworks |
| **Adviser** | Provide specific recommendations | High stakes; team has no viable options |

**Common mistake**: Defaulting to teacher or adviser when coaching would be more powerful. Ask: "Does this team need information, or do they need to discover something?"

## Team Maturity: Tuckman's Model

Teams move through predictable stages. Coaching interventions differ by stage:

**Forming** — Polite, cautious; roles unclear; avoiding conflict
- Coach: Help establish working agreements; surface expectations; create safety

**Storming** — Conflict emerges; roles blur; frustration visible
- Coach: Name what's happening; mediate; reinforce team purpose; don't smooth over conflict

**Norming** — Agreement forming; cohesion increasing; collaboration improving
- Coach: Reinforce good practices; encourage self-organisation; reduce presence

**Performing** — Seamless collaboration; high delivery; self-managing
- Coach: Maintain stretch goals; prevent complacency; prepare for Adjourning

**Adjourning** — Team disbands; transition needed
- Coach: Facilitate closure; knowledge transfer; celebrate accomplishments

## Coaching Conversations

Use the **GROW model** for structured coaching conversations:

- **G — Goal**: What do you want to achieve from this conversation?
- **R — Reality**: What is the current situation? What have you tried?
- **O — Options**: What could you do? What else? What would X do?
- **W — Will / Way Forward**: What will you do? By when? What support do you need?

**Powerful questions for agile coaches**:
- "What's stopping you from doing that right now?"
- "If you knew the answer, what would it be?"
- "What would success look like in 3 months?"
- "Who else is affected by this? Have you talked to them?"
- "What would you do if you weren't afraid of the outcome?"
- "What assumption are you making that might not be true?"

## Coaching Agreements

Establish a coaching agreement before engaging with a team or individual:

1. **Purpose**: What are we trying to achieve together?
2. **Scope**: What's in and out of bounds for coaching?
3. **Roles**: What will the coach do? What will the coachee do?
4. **Cadence**: How often? For how long?
5. **Measurement**: How will we know it's working?
6. **Boundaries**: Confidentiality? Reporting lines? What happens if it's not working?

Without an explicit agreement, coaching relationships often drift into consulting or management.

## Psychological Safety

Teams cannot learn, innovate, or give honest feedback without psychological safety (Amy Edmondson's work). Coaches must create and protect it.

**Indicators of low psychological safety**:
- No disagreement in meetings but lots of "corridor conversations"
- Retrospective feedback is bland or superficial
- People defer to the most senior voice automatically
- Mistakes are hidden rather than shared

**Interventions**:
- Model vulnerability — share your own mistakes and uncertainties
- Reward challenge — explicitly thank people who raise difficult truths
- Separate idea critique from person critique — "that approach..." not "you..."
- Create anonymous channels for feedback when safety is very low
- Use retrospective formats that allow anonymous input initially

## Systemic / Organisational Coaching

Team-level coaching has limits. Many team dysfunctions are symptoms of organisational design problems. Agile coaches working at enterprise level must be comfortable with:

- **Organisational design**: Congruence between team structure and value flow (Conway's Law)
- **Leadership coaching**: Helping leaders shift from command-and-control to servant leadership
- **Governance adaptation**: Changing funding models, stage-gates, and reporting to support agile delivery
- **Change management**: Using Kotter's 8-step, ADKAR, or other models for sustained transformation
- **Coalition building**: Identifying and growing internal champions at all levels

## Agile Coaching Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Description | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| Seagull coaching | Flies in, makes noise, flies out | Sustained presence; follow-through |
| Framework zealot | Prescribes one framework regardless of context | Assess context first; tailor |
| Comfort zone coach | Only works on easy problems | Address systemic impediments |
| Advice machine | Answers every question with a solution | Coach first; advise last |
| Dependency creator | Team can't work without coach present | Explicit exit strategy from day one |

## Measuring Coaching Impact

Track leading and lagging indicators:

**Leading**: Team health check trends, retrospective action completion rate, psychological safety survey scores, impediment resolution speed

**Lagging**: Delivery predictability, customer satisfaction, time-to-market, team stability/retention
