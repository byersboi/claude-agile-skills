# Scrum Master Mentoring

Senior Scrum Masters occupy a unique position: they are still practitioners, not managers, yet they carry responsibility for the quality and development of other SMs across teams or a programme. This is not line management. It is craft mentoring — passing on hard-won experience, pattern recognition, and professional judgement to people who are still building theirs.

Effective SM mentoring requires fluency across three modes: coaching (drawing out the other SM's own thinking), mentoring (sharing experience when it's genuinely useful), and consulting (giving a direct answer when time pressure demands it). Knowing which mode to be in — and why — is the core skill.

## Scrum Master Maturity Model

Before you can develop an SM, you need a shared understanding of what good looks like and where they are on the journey. Use maturity as a development lens, not a label.

**Level 1 — Ceremonial SM**
- Focuses on running the ceremonies correctly
- Sees the role as organiser and meeting facilitator
- Relies on the framework as a script
- Tends to report impediments rather than resolve them
- Development focus: Help them see that ceremonies are a means, not the job

**Level 2 — Team-Focused SM**
- Actively coaches the team on self-organisation
- Removes impediments within the team's boundary
- Understands the difference between facilitating and directing
- Still primarily inward-focused; limited organisational awareness
- Development focus: Expand their view to the system the team operates in

**Level 3 — Systemic SM**
- Works on organisational impediments as well as team-level ones
- Coaches the Product Owner and stakeholders, not just the team
- Understands and influences the broader delivery system
- Can hold their own in conversations with senior leaders
- Development focus: Coach at scale; develop others; operate strategically

**Level 4 — SM Leader / Mentor**
- Develops other SMs; runs or contributes to Communities of Practice
- Shapes agile culture beyond their immediate teams
- Challenges organisational dysfunction at source
- Acts as a thought partner to ADMs, Heads of Delivery, and leadership
- Development focus: Legacy — what capability does the organisation have when they leave?

Avoid using levels punitively. Use them as a map for conversation: "Where do you see yourself? Where do you want to go? What's the next step?"

## The Mentoring Relationship

**Contracting**: Establish the relationship intentionally. Agree:
- What does the mentee want to get from the relationship?
- What topics are in scope? What's out of scope?
- How often will you meet, for how long, in what format?
- What does the mentee commit to bringing? (specific situations, reflections, questions)
- How will you review and close the relationship when the time comes?

**The mentor's modes** — know which one you're in:

| Mode | When to use | What it sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| **Coaching** | The SM has the capability but needs to think it through | "What have you tried? What else could you do?" |
| **Mentoring** | They lack experience of this situation; your story is genuinely useful | "I've seen this before — here's what I found..." |
| **Consulting** | Direct answer needed; stakes are high; time is short | "In this situation, I'd do X because..." |
| **Challenging** | The SM is avoiding something difficult or telling themselves a story | "I notice you've described this twice. What's really going on?" |

The most common mistake senior SMs make in mentoring is defaulting to consulting — giving answers when coaching would serve the mentee's development better. Ask more than you tell.

**Building psychological safety in the relationship**: The mentee needs to feel safe bringing their real struggles — not just their successes. Model vulnerability. Share your own failures and what you learned from them. If every session is about wins, the relationship is too shallow.

## Mentoring Conversations

**Structuring a session** (typical 60-minute mentoring conversation):

1. **Check-in** (5 mins): How are they? What's the energy level? What's changed since last time?
2. **Agenda** (5 mins): What do they want to get out of today? What's most alive for them right now?
3. **Deep exploration** (40 mins): Work through one or two topics with depth. Resist the urge to cover everything.
4. **Synthesis** (5 mins): What are their key takeaways? What will they do differently?
5. **Commitments** (5 mins): Specific actions before the next session. Not a long list — one or two meaningful things.

**Powerful questions for SM mentoring**:
- "What do you think is really going on with that team?"
- "What would you do if you weren't worried about how it would land?"
- "Whose problem is this — the team's or yours?"
- "What are you tolerating that you shouldn't be?"
- "If you coached someone else in your situation, what would you tell them?"
- "What does the team need from you that they're not getting?"
- "What's the story you're telling yourself about why this isn't working?"
- "What would 'good enough' look like here?"

**Handling the 'just tell me what to do' request**: Mentees under pressure often want an answer. Resist immediately. First explore: "I can share what I've seen work, but I'm curious — what's your instinct?" If they genuinely have no frame of reference, then share your experience — but as a story to reflect on, not a prescription.

## Observing and Giving Feedback

One of the highest-value things a senior SM can do is observe another SM facilitating and give precise, specific feedback. This requires preparation, presence, and courage.

**Before observing**:
- Brief the mentee: what are they working on? What do they want feedback on specifically?
- Agree your role — silent observer, or will you have any involvement?
- Brief the team if appropriate — an unannounced observer changes group dynamics

**During observation — what to notice**:
- Does the SM have a clear outcome for the session? Are they navigating toward it?
- How do they use silence? Do they rush to fill it?
- Whose voices are dominant? Who isn't being heard? What does the SM do about it?
- Are they facilitating or directing? Asking or telling?
- How do they handle conflict or discomfort in the room?
- What is their body language communicating? Where do they position themselves?
- How do they close the session? Are decisions and actions clear?

**Giving feedback**:
- Use SBI (Situation, Behaviour, Impact): "In the retrospective, when the team went quiet after the difficult topic came up [Situation], you moved on to the next question [Behaviour] — the team didn't get to process what was in the room [Impact]."
- Lead with specific observation, not evaluation. "You seemed nervous" is less useful than "you spoke faster and avoided eye contact with the two people who were disagreeing."
- Ask before telling: "What did you notice? How did that feel to you?" Often the SM already knows.
- Balance: name what worked and why, not just what to improve.
- Prioritise: give two or three pieces of feedback, not ten. What's the most important thing for their development right now?

## SM Supervision

Supervision — borrowed from counselling and coaching professions — is structured reflective practice. It's distinct from mentoring: the focus is not on skill development but on the SM's professional and personal wellbeing, and on examining the SM's relationship with their work.

**Why SMs need supervision**: The SM role involves holding a lot for the team — absorbing stress, naming what's unspoken, navigating organisational dysfunction. Without a reflective outlet, SMs can burn out, become cynical, or project their own issues onto the team.

**Supervision formats**:

| Format | Description | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| 1:1 supervision | Senior SM and mentee; structured reflection on a specific situation | Depth; sensitive topics |
| Group supervision | 4–8 SMs; one brings a case; group reflects | Peer learning; diverse perspectives |
| Peer supervision | Two SMs of similar seniority; mutual reflection | When hierarchy is a barrier |

**Running a group supervision session** (90 minutes):
1. One SM presents a real situation (5–10 mins) — describe what happened, what they did, what the outcome was
2. The group asks clarifying questions only — no advice yet (10 mins)
3. The group reflects openly — "what this brings up for me is...", "I notice...", "I'm curious about..." (20 mins)
4. The presenting SM responds — what has landed? What new thinking do they have? (10 mins)
5. The group offers suggestions and experience (15 mins)
6. The presenting SM states their takeaways and commitments (5 mins)
7. Brief group debrief — what was it like to be in this session? (10 mins)

The discipline: no advice until invited. The instinct to help is strong; supervision requires sitting with the presenting SM's discomfort rather than rushing to resolve it.

## Community of Practice

An SM Community of Practice (CoP) is the mechanism for developing SM capability across an organisation at scale. Done well, it is a genuine learning community. Done badly, it becomes a weekly update meeting that everyone dreads.

**Purpose of an SM CoP**:
- Shared learning across teams and contexts
- Peer support and problem-solving
- Consistency of practice (without mandating uniformity)
- Surfacing systemic impediments that affect all SMs
- Professional development and identity

**Cadence**: Weekly is usually too frequent unless the organisation is in active transformation. Fortnightly or monthly with a focused agenda works better.

**CoP session formats** — rotate to maintain energy:

| Format | Description |
|---|---|
| Case study | One SM presents a challenge; group explores together (supervision-lite) |
| Practice share | An SM shares something that's working; group discusses applicability |
| Guest session | Invite an agile coach, leader, or external practitioner |
| Workshop | Skill-building on a specific topic (e.g., facilitation techniques, handling a difficult stakeholder) |
| Retrospective on the CoP | Regularly review the CoP itself — is it serving members? |
| Open space | Attendees set the agenda; self-organising discussion |

**What kills a CoP**:
- No clear purpose — it becomes a status meeting
- The same person always facilitates (rotate)
- Attendance is mandatory — autonomy matters; make it valuable enough that people choose to come
- Senior leaders dominate — SMs self-censor
- No follow-through — conversations produce no action or learning artefacts

**The senior SM's role in a CoP**: Facilitate sometimes, but don't always lead. Your job is to make the CoP self-sustaining. If it falls apart when you're absent, it's not yet a community.

## SM Career Development

**Having career conversations**: As a mentor, you are not responsible for the mentee's career — but you are a thought partner in it. Explore:
- What kind of SM do they want to be in two years?
- Do they want to go deeper (master practitioner) or broader (agile coach, delivery manager, product)?
- What experiences are they missing that would accelerate their growth?
- What's holding them back — skill, confidence, opportunity, or environment?

**SM career paths** — there is no single route:

| Direction | Description | Typical next step |
|---|---|---|
| Senior / Principal SM | Deeper craft; mentoring others; multiple teams | Lead SM, Head of Agile Delivery |
| Agile Coach | Broader scope; team and organisational coaching | Enterprise Agile Coach |
| Delivery Manager / ADM | More governance and stakeholder focus | Programme Manager, Head of Delivery |
| Product | Pivot to product ownership or management | Associate Product Manager, Product Owner |
| Specialist | Facilitation, training, consulting | Internal trainer, external consultant |

**Stretch assignments**: The fastest way to develop an SM is to put them in situations that require growth. Identify and offer:
- A difficult team they haven't worked with before
- A high-stakes ceremony (quarterly planning, programme retrospective)
- Representing SMs in a leadership forum
- Co-facilitating an SM CoP session
- Mentoring a newer SM themselves

## Common Development Challenges

**"My team doesn't respect me"**: Usually a symptom of the SM being too directive, too passive, or not creating enough value. Explore: What does the team actually need right now? What is the SM doing (or not doing) that creates the dynamic? Avoid rescuing — help them find their own answer.

**The SM who can't let go**: Fixes problems instead of coaching the team to solve them. Often driven by anxiety or a need to be needed. Name the pattern; explore what they're afraid of. The team's dependency on their SM is not a success metric.

**The SM who avoids conflict**: Runs smooth ceremonies but never names the elephant in the room. Explore their relationship with discomfort. Use low-stakes practice: name a tension in the mentoring conversation itself and observe how they respond.

**The SM who's gone native**: Over-identifies with the team; loses their systemic perspective; advocates for the team at the expense of honest challenge. Help them reconnect with their accountability to the whole system, not just the team they love.

**The SM who's burned out**: Has been absorbing the team's dysfunction without outlet. They need supervision more than skill development. Help them identify what they're carrying and what they need to put down.

**The SM who wants to be a manager**: Uses the role as a waiting room for line management. Their coaching is actually performance management in disguise. Have an honest conversation about role clarity and career direction.
