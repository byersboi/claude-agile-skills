# Facilitation

Facilitation is the art of helping groups do their best thinking together. A facilitator is neutral on content and active on process — they do not advocate for outcomes, they create the conditions for the group to reach their own.

Facilitation is the primary skill underlying all agile ceremonies. See related skills:
- **`scrum`** — Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective
- **`agile-ceremonies`** — Backlog Refinement, Story Mapping, Team Charter, Inception
- **`agile-coach`** — Coaching conversations and psychological safety
- **`conflict-management`** — Facilitating sessions where conflict is present

## The Facilitator's Stance

**Neutral on content, active on process**: The facilitator does not have a preferred outcome. They serve the group's thinking, not their own agenda.

**Key responsibilities**:
- Design the session structure (agenda, timing, methods)
- Create psychological safety for honest participation
- Ensure all voices are heard — not just the loudest
- Keep the group focused on the stated purpose
- Make group thinking visible (walls, boards, shared documents)
- Protect the process, not the outcome

**When to step out of the facilitator role**: If you have a strong view on the content, declare it and step back from facilitation for that topic. Trying to be both facilitator and advocate destroys trust in the process.

## Session Design

### The PODA Framework

Before designing any session, answer:
- **P — Purpose**: Why are we meeting? What problem are we solving?
- **O — Outcomes**: What will participants leave with? (decisions, shared understanding, actions, a plan)
- **D — Design**: What sequence of activities will get us there?
- **A — Agenda**: How long does each activity take? What materials are needed?

A clear purpose is non-negotiable. Without it, sessions drift and participants disengage.

### Diverge–Converge Pattern

Most productive group sessions follow this shape:

1. **Diverge** — Generate options, ideas, perspectives without judgement
2. **Emerge** — Patterns surface through sharing and clustering
3. **Converge** — Evaluate, prioritise, decide

**Common mistake**: Converging before adequate divergence. This produces anchoring and groupthink. Always allow individual thinking before group discussion.

### Silent Writing First

Always give 3–8 minutes of individual, silent writing before group discussion. Benefits:
- Prevents anchoring to the first speaker
- Amplifies quieter voices (introverts, junior members)
- Produces more diverse ideas
- Speeds up discussion (less time restating perspectives aloud)

## Core Facilitation Techniques

### 1-2-4-All (Liberating Structures)

**Purpose**: Generate ideas and build shared understanding at scale without losing individual perspectives.

**How**:
1. Think alone (1 min)
2. Share in pairs — build on each other (2 min)
3. Share in groups of 4 — what is the same? What's new? (4 min)
4. Groups share key insights with all (5 min)

**Use**: Opening a topic, generating options, checking for shared understanding after a presentation.

### Dot Voting

**Purpose**: Prioritise from a large set of options quickly.

**How**: Each participant gets N dots (typically 3–5). Place dots on the options you most value. Count. Discuss top results before finalising — dot votes surface preference, not always wisdom.

**Anti-pattern**: Using dot votes to make final decisions without discussion. Votes reveal priorities; the conversation reveals whether those priorities are sound.

### Fist of Five (Consent Decision Making)

**Purpose**: Gauge group agreement and surface concerns before finalising a decision.

**Scale**:
- **5** — Strong support; will champion this
- **4** — Support; happy to proceed
- **3** — Mild support; can live with it; willing to let the group decide
- **2** — Concerns; need them addressed before I can support
- **1** — Serious concerns; need significant changes
- **0 / Fist** — Block; cannot support; fundamental objection

**How**: Call the question clearly. Everyone shows simultaneously. If anyone shows ≤2, hear their concern before proceeding. A block (0) is rare and should be treated seriously.

**Use**: Closing discussions on significant decisions; checking readiness to proceed; working agreements.

### Round Robin

**Purpose**: Ensure every voice is heard before open discussion.

**How**: Go around the group in order. Each person speaks once (or passes) before anyone speaks again. Facilitator captures key points visibly.

**Use**: Check-ins, opening a topic, ensuring quieter participants are heard, closing reflections.

### Affinity Clustering

**Purpose**: Organise a large set of ideas into themes.

**How**:
1. Ideas written on individual stickies (one idea per sticky)
2. Participants silently move stickies into groups of similar ideas
3. Facilitate discussion only where placement is genuinely disputed
4. Name the clusters collaboratively

**Use**: Retrospectives, workshop outputs, backlog themes, risk identification.

### Parking Lot

**Purpose**: Capture valuable but off-topic contributions without derailing the session.

**How**: Maintain a visible "Parking Lot" area on the board. When an important but out-of-scope topic surfaces, write it there and acknowledge it: "That's important — let's capture it in the Parking Lot and decide at the end whether it needs its own session."

**Close each session** by reviewing the Parking Lot: action, schedule, or discard each item.

## Advanced Techniques

### World Café

**Purpose**: Enable a large group to have deep, cross-pollinating conversations.

**How**:
1. Set up "tables" (physical or virtual) with a question per table
2. Groups discuss the question (20 min); one person stays as "host"
3. Groups rotate; host shares previous conversation; new group builds on it
4. Final harvest: host shares synthesis with all

**Use**: Large workshops (15–100 people); exploring complex topics from multiple perspectives.

### Open Space Technology

**Purpose**: Self-organised agenda for large groups around a theme.

**How**:
1. One overarching question or theme
2. Participants propose sessions — written on cards, posted to a grid (time × location)
3. Each proposer facilitates their session
4. Law of Two Feet: participants go where they contribute and learn

**Use**: Conferences, all-hands sessions, innovation days, large retrospectives.

### Fishbowl

**Purpose**: Enable structured conversation between a small group while a larger group observes.

**How**:
1. 4–5 chairs in the centre (the "fishbowl"); remaining participants observe
2. Inner group discusses the topic
3. One chair is left empty — any observer can take it, and one inner-circle member must leave
4. Rotate as needed

**Use**: Exploring a contentious topic; modelling good dialogue; large groups where not everyone can speak simultaneously.

## Remote Facilitation

Remote sessions require more structure and explicit facilitation than in-person. Key adaptations:

**Before the session**:
- Share purpose, outcomes, and agenda in advance
- Confirm tooling (Miro, MURAL, Jamboard, Mentimeter, etc.) and test it
- Design activities that work asynchronously first (async voting, silent writing in a shared doc)

**During the session**:
- Open with a verbal check-in (names + one word) — breaks the audio silence
- Use breakout rooms for 1-2-4-All and small group work
- Make everything visible on a shared digital board
- Call people by name to include quieter participants
- Use polling tools (Mentimeter, Slido) for anonymous input where safety is low

**Energy management**:
- No more than 90 minutes without a break
- Vary activity types — don't lecture for more than 10 minutes
- Use the chat channel actively; acknowledge contributions

## Facilitation Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Description | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| Advocacy disguised as facilitation | Steering towards your preferred outcome | Separate roles; declare your view openly |
| Only the loud voices | Dominant speakers control the output | Round robin, silent writing, breakouts |
| Death by discussion | Hours of talk, no decisions | Timeboxing, explicit decision methods |
| Skipping divergence | Jump straight to "so what should we do?" | Mandate silent writing before discussion |
| No visible output | Discussion happens but nothing is captured | Record everything on a visible board |
| Ignoring energy | Pushing through fatigue | Build in breaks; read the room |
| Fake parking lot | Items go in, never come out | Review and action the parking lot at the end |

## Facilitation Planning Checklist

Before every session:
- [ ] Purpose and outcomes written and shared
- [ ] Agenda with timings designed
- [ ] Methods chosen for each segment (diverge before converge)
- [ ] Room/virtual setup arranged (boards, tools, breakout rooms)
- [ ] Materials prepared (stickies, cards, templates, polls)
- [ ] Roles clarified (timekeeper, note-taker, tech support if remote)
- [ ] Parking lot space reserved

After every session:
- [ ] Outputs captured and distributed
- [ ] Actions documented with owners and dates
- [ ] Parking lot reviewed and actioned
- [ ] Facilitator retrospective (what worked, what to improve)
