# Agile Foundations

Agile is a mindset for developing products and delivering value in uncertain, complex environments. It emerged from software development but applies broadly to any knowledge work. The foundation is empirical process control: make decisions based on observation and experimentation, not upfront planning.

## The Agile Manifesto (2001)

Seventeen practitioners signed the Manifesto for Agile Software Development at Snowbird, Utah. They identified four value pairs — the left side is valued **more** than the right, not exclusively:

1. **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools
2. **Working software** over comprehensive documentation
3. **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation
4. **Responding to change** over following a plan

The right-side items still have value. Agile does not eliminate process, documentation, contracts, or plans — it changes their relative priority and purpose.

## The 12 Principles

The principles behind the Manifesto give practical guidance:

1. Deliver valuable software early and continuously — customer satisfaction is the highest priority
2. Welcome changing requirements, even late in development — harness change for competitive advantage
3. Deliver working software frequently (weeks, not months) — prefer shorter timescales
4. Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project
5. Build around motivated individuals — give them the environment, support, and trust they need
6. Face-to-face conversation is the most efficient and effective method of conveying information
7. Working software is the primary measure of progress
8. Promote sustainable development — sponsors, developers, and users should maintain a constant pace indefinitely
9. Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility
10. Simplicity — the art of maximising the amount of work not done — is essential
11. The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organising teams
12. At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behaviour

## Three Pillars of Empiricism

Agile frameworks (especially Scrum) are grounded in empirical process control:

- **Transparency** — shared understanding of the process and outcomes; common language
- **Inspection** — frequent examination of progress toward goals and detection of undesirable variance
- **Adaptation** — adjusting processes and plans when inspection reveals problems

All agile events are inspection-and-adaptation opportunities. Ceremonies that don't result in adaptation are ceremonies for ceremony's sake.

## Agile vs Waterfall

| Aspect | Waterfall | Agile |
|---|---|---|
| Planning | Comprehensive upfront | Rolling; just-enough |
| Requirements | Fixed at start | Evolving |
| Delivery | End of project | Continuously |
| Feedback | Late | Frequent |
| Risk management | Front-loaded analysis | Managed through iteration |
| Change response | Change control process | Expected; welcomed |
| Success metric | On-time, on-budget | Value delivered |

Neither is universally better. Waterfall suits known, stable, low-uncertainty work. Agile suits complex, uncertain, exploratory work (Cynefin: complicated vs. complex domains).

## Agile Frameworks at a Glance

| Framework | Scale | Key Strength | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scrum | Team (≤10) | Structured cadence; roles clarity | Product development |
| Kanban | Team | Flow optimisation; continuous delivery | Operations, support, mixed work |
| XP | Team | Engineering practices | High-quality software |
| SAFe | Program/Portfolio | Enterprise alignment | Large organisations |
| LeSS | Programme | Simplicity at scale | Feature teams across products |
| Nexus | Programme | Scrum at scale (3-9 teams) | Multi-team products |
| Spotify | Organisation | Autonomy + alignment | Engineering culture |
| DSDM | Programme | Business involvement; time-boxing | Business-driven projects |

## Agile Adoption Patterns

**Common failure modes**:
- **ScrumBut**: "We do Scrum, but without retrospectives / with fixed scope / without a real PO"
- **Cargo cult agile**: Following the ceremonies without the mindset; "zombie scrum"
- **Agile theatre**: Stickies and standups masking command-and-control management
- **Scaling too early**: Adding SAFe/LeSS before team-level agility is working

**Indicators of genuine agility**:
- Teams regularly ship working product
- Retrospectives produce real, implemented changes
- Stakeholders engage in reviews rather than receiving reports
- Teams pull their own work; management enables rather than directs
- Failure is treated as learning, not blame

## Coaching Agile Adoption

When coaching a team or organisation new to agile:

1. **Start with why** — connect agile to business problems, not methodology preference
2. **Assess current state** — use a team health check or maturity model before prescribing
3. **Choose the minimum viable framework** — start with fewer ceremonies, add as needed
4. **Inspect and adapt the adoption itself** — retrospect on how the adoption is going
5. **Identify champions** — find people who are already working this way; amplify them
6. **Address the organisational system** — agile teams in non-agile organisations face structural impediments; tackle governance, funding, and HR alongside team practices
