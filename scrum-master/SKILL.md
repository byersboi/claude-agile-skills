# Scrum Master

The Scrum Master is accountable for the Scrum Team's effectiveness. They do this by enabling the team to improve its practices within the Scrum framework, removing impediments, and helping everyone understand Scrum theory and practice — both within the team and in the broader organisation.

The Scrum Master is not a project manager, not a team lead, and not a meeting organiser. They are a servant leader and change agent.

## The Eight Stances

Effective Scrum Masters move fluidly between stances depending on the situation:

1. **Servant Leader** — Puts the team's needs first; removes obstacles; creates conditions for success
2. **Facilitator** — Structures and guides collaborative events without dominating them
3. **Coach** — Uses powerful questions to help the team discover their own answers
4. **Mentor** — Shares experience and guidance when the team lacks specific knowledge
5. **Change Agent** — Challenges the status quo; drives organisational improvement
6. **Impediment Remover** — Identifies and resolves blockers that exceed the team's self-organising capability
7. **Teacher** — Educates team, stakeholders, and management on Scrum values and practices
8. **Process Authority** — Guards the integrity of the Scrum framework; protects events from being undermined

## Serving the Scrum Team

**Coaching the team on self-organisation**: The goal is for the team to need the Scrum Master less over time, not more. Resist solving problems for the team. Ask "What do you think you should do?" before offering solutions.

**Facilitating events effectively**: Prepare for each event with a clear purpose, format, and expected outcome. Create safety for participation. Manage time. Ensure decisions are recorded and followed up.

**Supporting the Product Owner**: Help with effective backlog management, stakeholder communication, and understanding the importance of a clear product goal. The SM does not own the backlog.

**Building team norms**: Work with the team to establish a working agreement covering communication, decision-making, availability, and quality standards.

## Impediment Management

**Distinguish impediment types**:
- **Team-solvable**: The team can resolve it — coach them to do so
- **SM-solvable**: Requires organisational access or relationships the team lacks — SM acts
- **Systemic**: Requires sustained organisational change — SM escalates and advocates

**Impediment log**: Maintain a visible, prioritised list of impediments. Make blockers transparent to stakeholders. Track resolution time as a metric.

**Escalation path**: For systemic impediments, name the issue, quantify the impact, propose the change, and escalate to the right authority. Don't absorb impediments — surface them.

**Common impediment sources**:
- Unclear or absent Product Owner
- Dependencies on external teams without SLAs
- Technical debt preventing sustainable pace
- Organisational policies conflicting with self-organisation
- Missing infrastructure or tooling

## Serving the Organisation

The Scrum Master's organisational work is often the hardest and most impactful:

- **Lead and coach adoption** — Help leaders understand agile; reshape governance to support teams
- **Plan Scrum implementations** — Guide how Scrum is applied across different contexts
- **Remove barriers** — Work with management to change structures that limit team effectiveness
- **Support cultural change** — Model transparency, courage, and empiricism in every interaction

## Facilitation Techniques

**Diverge-then-converge**: Generate options individually before discussing as a group. Prevents anchoring and groupthink.

**Liberating Structures**: Use formats like 1-2-4-All, TRIZ, or Troika Consulting for more inclusive facilitation.

**Fist-to-five**: Consent-based decision making — ask participants to hold up fingers (0=block, 1-2=concerns, 3=neutral, 4-5=support). Surfaces concerns without requiring full consensus.

**Silent voting**: Post options; participants dot-vote simultaneously. Reduces social conformity bias.

**Timeboxing**: Set explicit time limits for each agenda item. Honour them. Overtime in one item steals time from others.

## Coaching the Team's Health

Use regular team health checks to track trajectory, not just point-in-time state. Spotify's Squad Health Check, the Agile Health Radar, or bespoke models all work — consistency matters more than format.

Focus areas:
- **Psychological safety** — Are people speaking up?
- **Clarity of purpose** — Does the team understand the product goal?
- **Self-organisation** — Is the team pulling work and making decisions?
- **Technical practices** — Is quality improving?
- **Stakeholder relationships** — Are reviews collaborative?

## Scrum Master Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Description | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Scrum Police | Enforces rules without explaining why | Lead with principles, not rules |
| Proxy PO | Takes over Product Owner decisions | Coach the PO; don't absorb their role |
| Note Taker | Only manages logistics; no coaching | Build facilitation and coaching skills |
| Impediment Absorber | Takes ownership of all problems | Coach team to solve what they can |
| Sprint Cop | Enforces sprint scope rigidly | Focus on Sprint Goal, not item list |
| Meeting Chair | Runs meetings but doesn't improve them | Focus on event purpose and outcomes |

## Metrics for the Scrum Master

The SM should not be measured on team velocity (that creates pressure to game it). Instead, track:

- Impediment resolution time (average days to resolve)
- Number of impediments raised vs. resolved per sprint
- Team health check trends over time
- Retrospective action completion rate
- Frequency of stakeholder participation in Sprint Reviews
