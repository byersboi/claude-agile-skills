# Scrum Master

The Scrum Master is accountable for the Scrum Team's effectiveness. They do this by enabling the team to improve its practices within the Scrum framework, removing impediments, and helping everyone understand Scrum theory and practice — both within the team and in the broader organisation.

The Scrum Master is not a project manager, not a team lead, and not a meeting organiser. They are a servant leader and change agent.

## The Eight Stances

Effective Scrum Masters move fluidly between stances depending on the situation:

1. **Servant Leader** — Puts the team's needs first; removes obstacles; creates conditions for success
2. **Facilitator** — Structures and guides collaborative events without dominating them
3. **Coach** — Uses powerful questions to help the team discover their own answers
4. **Mentor** — Shares experience and guidance when the team lacks specific knowledge
5. **Change Agent** — Challenges the status quo; drives organisational improvement
6. **Impediment Remover** — Identifies and resolves blockers that exceed the team's self-organising capability
7. **Teacher** — Educates team, stakeholders, and management on Scrum values and practices
8. **Process Authority** — Guards the integrity of the Scrum framework; protects events from being undermined

## Serving the Scrum Team

**Coaching the team on self-organisation**: The goal is for the team to need the Scrum Master less over time, not more. Resist solving problems for the team. Ask "What do you think you should do?" before offering solutions.

**Facilitating events effectively**: Prepare for each event with a clear purpose, format, and expected outcome. Create safety for participation. Manage time. Ensure decisions are recorded and followed up.

**Supporting the Product Owner**: Help with effective backlog management, stakeholder communication, and understanding the importance of a clear product goal. The SM does not own the backlog.

**Building team norms**: Work with the team to establish a working agreement covering communication, decision-making, availability, and quality standards.

## Impediment Management

**Distinguish impediment types**:
- **Team-solvable**: The team can resolve it — coach them to do so
- **SM-solvable**: Requires organisational access or relationships the team lacks — SM acts
- **Systemic**: Requires sustained organisational change — SM escalates and advocates

**Impediment log**: Maintain a visible, prioritised list of impediments. Make blockers transparent to stakeholders. Track resolution time as a metric.

**Escalation path**: For systemic impediments, name the issue, quantify the impact, propose the change, and escalate to the right authority. Don't absorb impediments — surface them.

**Common impediment sources**:
- Unclear or absent Product Owner
- Dependencies on external teams without SLAs
- Technical debt preventing sustainable pace
- Organisational policies conflicting with self-organisation
- Missing infrastructure or tooling

## Serving the Organisation

The Scrum Master's organisational work is often the hardest and most impactful:

- **Lead and coach adoption** — Help leaders understand agile; reshape governance to support teams
- **Plan Scrum implementations** — Guide how Scrum is applied across different contexts
- **Remove barriers** — Work with management to change structures that limit team effectiveness
- **Support cultural change** — Model transparency, courage, and empiricism in every interaction

## Facilitation Techniques

**Diverge-then-converge**: Generate options individually before discussing as a group. Prevents anchoring and groupthink.

**Liberating Structures**: Use formats like 1-2-4-All, TRIZ, or Troika Consulting for more inclusive facilitation.

**Fist-to-five**: Consent-based decision making — ask participants to hold up fingers (0=block, 1-2=concerns, 3=neutral, 4-5=support). Surfaces concerns without requiring full consensus.

**Silent voting**: Post options; participants dot-vote simultaneously. Reduces social conformity bias.

**Timeboxing**: Set explicit time limits for each agenda item. Honour them. Overtime in one item steals time from others.

## Coaching the Team's Health

Use regular team health checks to track trajectory, not just point-in-time state. Spotify's Squad Health Check, the Agile Health Radar, or bespoke models all work — consistency matters more than format.

Focus areas:
- **Psychological safety** — Are people speaking up?
- **Clarity of purpose** — Does the team understand the product goal?
- **Self-organisation** — Is the team pulling work and making decisions?
- **Technical practices** — Is quality improving?
- **Stakeholder relationships** — Are reviews collaborative?

## Scrum Master Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Description | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Scrum Police | Enforces rules without explaining why | Lead with principles, not rules |
| Proxy PO | Takes over Product Owner decisions | Coach the PO; don't absorb their role |
| Note Taker | Only manages logistics; no coaching | Build facilitation and coaching skills |
| Impediment Absorber | Takes ownership of all problems | Coach team to solve what they can |
| Sprint Cop | Enforces sprint scope rigidly | Focus on Sprint Goal, not item list |
| Meeting Chair | Runs meetings but doesn't improve them | Focus on event purpose and outcomes |

## Metrics for the Scrum Master

The SM should not be measured on team velocity (that creates pressure to game it). Instead, track:

- Impediment resolution time (average days to resolve)
- Number of impediments raised vs. resolved per sprint
- Team health check trends over time
- Retrospective action completion rate
- Frequency of stakeholder participation in Sprint Reviews

## Joining a New Team: SM Onboarding

The first few weeks in a new team are critical. The temptation is to fix things quickly; the better move is to observe, listen, and build trust before changing anything.

### The First Two Weeks — Listen Before You Act

**Who to meet**:
- Every team member individually — understand their background, how long they've been on the team, what they find challenging, what's working
- The Product Owner — understand the product, the backlog's current state, and how the PO and team interact
- The line manager or engineering manager — understand expectations, history, and any concerns about the team
- Key stakeholders — anyone the team regularly depends on or delivers for
- Other Scrum Masters — how agile works in this organisation; informal norms and known landmines

**What to observe in ceremonies**:
- Who speaks and who doesn't in standups, planning, and retros
- The quality of backlog items — are they outcome-oriented or vague feature requests?
- How conflict and disagreement are handled — or avoided
- Whether retrospective actions get completed
- The team's relationship with the PO — collaborative or transactional?

**What not to do**:
- Don't change the ceremony format in week one
- Don't tell the team how their previous SM did things differently
- Don't propose solutions to problems before you've fully understood them
- Don't start reporting on the team to management before you have the team's trust

**Document what you learn**: Take notes on what's working, what's fragile, and what seems broken. This becomes your initial improvement backlog. Don't act on it immediately — validate it through conversations first.

### Setting Expectations with the Team

Within the first two weeks, have an explicit conversation with the team about what the Scrum Master role is and how you'll work together. This prevents common misunderstandings and gives the team a chance to shape the working relationship.

**What to cover**:

- **What you're accountable for**: Helping the team improve; removing impediments that exceed the team's reach; facilitating events; coaching on agile practices. You are not a project manager, note-taker, or task-assigner.
- **What you're not accountable for**: Delivery. The team delivers. You create conditions for them to do so effectively.
- **How you'll work**: Regular 1:1s if appropriate; your approach to retrospectives; how you'll handle impediments.
- **Your style**: Coaching-first, not prescriptive. You'll ask questions more than give answers. You'll push back sometimes — constructively.
- **What you need from them**: Transparency. Raise problems early. Tell you when something isn't working, including how you're showing up.

**A useful framing**: "My job is to make this team so effective that eventually you need me less, not more."

**With management**: Set expectations separately with your line manager or programme lead. Clarify how much you will and won't report upward about the team, and why. Protecting team confidentiality is essential to your effectiveness — this conversation is worth having early.

## The Scrum Master's Own Backlog

Effective Scrum Masters treat their own development work the way they treat team work: with discipline, prioritisation, and visibility.

An SM backlog is a personal list of improvements, experiments, and commitments — ordered by impact, reviewed regularly, and actually worked through rather than accumulated.

**What goes on the SM backlog**:
- Impediments being tracked and actively pursued
- Facilitation techniques to try in upcoming ceremonies
- Coaching approaches to try with specific individuals
- Learning items (books, courses, communities of practice)
- Organisational changes to advocate for
- Retrospective formats to experiment with
- Metrics or health check improvements to run

**How to maintain it**:
- Review weekly — keep it short enough to be real (5–10 active items)
- Prioritise by impact, not urgency — the loudest thing is not always the most important
- Mark progress — what's in flight, what's done, what's been deferred and why
- Reflect on it in your own retrospective — what did you commit to last sprint? What actually happened?

**Why it matters**: An SM who doesn't manage their own work systematically often creates the same dysfunction they're trying to coach the team out of — backlogs that grow without being worked, action items that drift, priorities driven by whoever shouted last.

## Scrum Self-Assessment

An SM can use a structured self-assessment to identify which aspects of Scrum are working well and where the team needs coaching attention. This is not a compliance audit — it is a diagnostic tool.

Run this assessment after joining a new team, quarterly, or when you sense something is off but can't pinpoint it.

### Roles

| Area | Healthy | Needs attention |
|---|---|---|
| Product Owner | PO makes prioritisation decisions; is available for the team; attends all events | PO delegates decisions upward; frequently absent; unclear on Product Goal |
| Scrum Master | SM coaches rather than directs; is growing the team's independence | SM is running meetings but not improving them; team depends on SM for decisions |
| Developers | Team self-organises; pulls work and raises impediments | Team waits to be assigned work; impediments are hidden not raised |
| PO / Dev relationship | Collaborative; team understands user need behind stories | Transactional; team builds what's written without questioning why |

### Artifacts

| Area | Healthy | Needs attention |
|---|---|---|
| Product Backlog | Ordered; top items are refined and understood; connects to Product Goal | Unsorted; too large; items lack acceptance criteria; no clear Product Goal |
| Sprint Backlog | Team owns it; updated daily; connected to Sprint Goal | SM or PO owns it; rarely updated; Sprint Goal is vague or missing |
| Increment | Something usable is shipped every sprint; Definition of Done is met | Sprints end with incomplete work; DoD is unclear or inconsistently applied |
| Definition of Done | Team-owned; enforced; evolves as quality improves | Doesn't exist; ignored under pressure; varies by team member |

### Events

| Area | Healthy | Needs attention |
|---|---|---|
| Sprint Planning | Team selects work based on capacity; Sprint Goal is clear; PO is engaged | Over-committed every sprint; Sprint Goal is vague; planning runs long with no outcome |
| Daily Scrum | Team inspects progress toward Sprint Goal; raises blockers immediately | Status report to SM; not actionable; skipped when SM is absent |
| Backlog Refinement | Regular cadence; items are refined before Sprint Planning; team involved | Happens sporadically; items arrive at planning unrefined; PO does it alone |
| Sprint Review | Stakeholders attend and give feedback; backlog updated based on learning | Demo to an empty room; no feedback collected; backlog unchanged after review |
| Retrospective | Actions are specific and followed up; topics go beyond process tweaks | Generates the same action items repeatedly; team compliance not engagement |

Use the assessment to identify 2–3 focus areas — not to fix everything at once. A single well-implemented improvement compounds over time.
