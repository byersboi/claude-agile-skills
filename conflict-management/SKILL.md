# Conflict Management

Conflict is a natural and necessary part of team life. The goal is not to eliminate conflict but to move teams from unproductive conflict (avoidance, personal attacks, political manoeuvring) toward productive conflict (open debate of ideas, honest disagreement, creative tension).

Healthy teams disagree — they just do it safely and constructively.

## Conflict Is Not the Problem

Patrick Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions of a Team places "Fear of Conflict" as the second dysfunction — built on absence of trust. Teams that avoid conflict don't actually eliminate it; they push it underground where it causes more damage.

**Productive conflict**: Debate about ideas, approaches, priorities. Passionate, honest, focused on the work.

**Unproductive conflict**: Personal attacks, political manoeuvring, passive aggression, silence-then-resentment, blame.

The Scrum Master's and coach's job is to create conditions for productive conflict and interrupt unproductive patterns.

## Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Modes

Five styles for responding to conflict, defined by two axes: assertiveness (pursuing your own concerns) and cooperativeness (satisfying others' concerns).

| Mode | Assertiveness | Cooperativeness | When It's Appropriate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Competing** | High | Low | Emergencies; unpopular but necessary decisions; when you know you're right and stakes are high |
| **Collaborating** | High | High | Complex problems needing both parties' insights; when commitment from both sides is essential |
| **Compromising** | Medium | Medium | When a quick resolution is needed and both parties have equal power; as a temporary fix |
| **Avoiding** | Low | Low | When the issue is trivial; when you need time to gather information; when emotions are too high to engage productively |
| **Accommodating** | Low | High | When you realise you are wrong; when the issue matters much more to the other party; to build goodwill |

**Most people have a default mode** — the one they reach for regardless of context. Help team members recognise their defaults and expand their range.

**The most underused mode in teams**: Collaborating. It takes more time but produces better outcomes and stronger commitment.

## The Conflict Escalation Ladder (Glasl)

Conflict escalates through stages if not addressed. Recognising the stage determines the right intervention.

**Stage 1 — Hardening**: Positions harden; people stop listening; occasional clashes.
→ *Intervention*: Direct conversation; active listening; clarify interests vs. positions

**Stage 2 — Debate/Polemics**: Discussions become arguments; "winning" matters more than truth.
→ *Intervention*: Facilitated dialogue; reframe around shared goals

**Stage 3 — Actions, not words**: People act to pressure rather than persuade; coalitions form.
→ *Intervention*: Mediation; structure problem-solving; separate people from positions

**Stage 4 — Images and coalitions**: Fixed stereotypes; "we vs. they" mentality; reputation attacks.
→ *Intervention*: External mediator; leadership involvement; may need structural changes

**Stage 5–9 (Win-Lose → Mutual Destruction)**: Beyond self-help; requires HR, legal, or leadership intervention.

**For agile practitioners**: Most team conflicts sit at stages 1–3. Intervene early. Conflict rarely de-escalates on its own.

## The Conflict Conversation Framework

For facilitating a direct conflict conversation between two or more parties:

### Preparation
- Meet individually with each party first
- Understand their position (what they want) and their interests (why they want it)
- Establish ground rules before the joint conversation: one person speaks at a time; attack the problem not the person; speak from your own experience ("I felt..." not "You always...")

### The Conversation

**1. Open**: Acknowledge the conflict directly and state the shared goal.
> "We're here because there's been tension between you both about [topic]. I want to help us understand what's going on and find a way forward that works for the team."

**2. Each party shares their perspective** (uninterrupted):
- Use the SBI model: **Situation** → **Behaviour** → **Impact**
- "In the last sprint review [Situation], when the decision was made without consulting the team [Behaviour], I felt excluded and it undermined my confidence in the plan [Impact]"

**3. Check understanding**: Each party reflects back what they heard — not whether they agree.
> "What I heard you say is... Is that right?"

**4. Identify interests beneath positions**: Ask "What's important to you about that?" until you reach underlying needs (respect, autonomy, fairness, clarity).

**5. Generate options**: Diverge before converging. "What could we do differently?" — brainstorm without evaluating.

**6. Agree on next steps**: Specific, observable, time-bound. Write them down.

## SBI Feedback Model

The most reliable structure for giving difficult feedback without triggering defensiveness.

- **S — Situation**: Describe the specific situation. "In yesterday's sprint planning..."
- **B — Behaviour**: Describe the observable behaviour, not the interpretation. "...you interrupted the team three times when they were estimating..."
- **I — Impact**: Describe the impact on you, the team, or the work. "...which meant quieter members stopped contributing and we may have underestimated the risk."

**What to avoid**:
- Generalisations: "You always do this" / "You never listen"
- Labels: "You're being difficult" / "You're not a team player"
- Interpretations: "You were trying to undermine the process"

**Follow SBI with a question**: "What was going on for you at that point?" — creates dialogue rather than judgement.

## Common Conflict Scenarios in Agile Teams

### PO vs. Development Team (Scope / Estimates)

**Pattern**: PO pushes for more scope; team pushes back; tension builds.
**Root cause**: Usually a shared delivery pressure that neither party owns fully.
**Intervention**: Facilitate a shared understanding of the Sprint Goal; make trade-offs visible; help PO understand team capacity constraints through data (velocity, impediment log).

### SM Perceived as Management Spy

**Pattern**: Team doesn't trust the SM; withholds information; uses SM as a status-report channel.
**Root cause**: SM was positioned as a manager or reporter by leadership; team has no safety.
**Intervention**: Re-contract the SM's role with the team; demonstrate confidentiality; coach leadership on appropriate interactions with the team.

### Two Strong Personalities Clash

**Pattern**: Two team members consistently disagree; others disengage; team splits into camps.
**Root cause**: Often a difference in values or working style; occasionally a specific past incident.
**Intervention**: Individual coaching with each; establish working agreements that address the specific flashpoints; use conflict conversation framework if needed.

### Remote Team Conflict (Easier to Misread)

**Pattern**: Async communication is misinterpreted; frustration builds invisibly; erupts in a call.
**Root cause**: Lack of tone/body language in text; assumptions about intent.
**Intervention**: Move conflict conversations to video; establish communication norms (response times, channel norms); create deliberate social connection.

### Conflict Avoidance (The Silent Killer)

**Pattern**: Team is "always fine"; retrospectives produce nothing; same issues recur; people complain offline.
**Root cause**: Low psychological safety; Groupthink; past experience of consequences for speaking up.
**Intervention**: Use anonymous input first; model vulnerability; introduce retrospective formats that create distance from personal views (e.g., Team Radar, KALM); reward honest challenge.

## When to Escalate

Escalate to management or HR when:
- Conflict involves harassment, discrimination, or a power imbalance that makes peer resolution impossible
- The conflict has reached Glasl stage 4+ (fixed images, coalition building, reputation attacks)
- Agreed resolutions are repeatedly broken
- Safety of team members is at risk

Document: what was observed, when, what interventions were tried, and what the impact was.

## Conflict and the Scrum Master

The SM holds a unique position: they have no formal authority, so they cannot mandate resolution. Their tools are:

- **Making conflict visible**: Naming what's happening in the room that everyone can see but no one is saying
- **Creating safety**: Ensuring the retrospective and daily interactions are safe enough for honest challenge
- **Facilitating**: Structuring conversations so both parties feel heard before moving to solutions
- **Coaching**: Helping individuals recognise their patterns and expand their range
- **Protecting**: Shielding the team from external pressure that creates internal tension

**What the SM should not do**: Take sides; resolve the conflict for the parties; absorb conflict by keeping it from surfacing; report private team conflict to management without consent.
